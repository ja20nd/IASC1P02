Film Review: WIND RIVER

Wind River

Review by Joel Antony

Taylor Sheridan's film "Wind River" (2017) recounts the story of a wildlife officer with a terrible history who pairs up with an FBI agent to investigate a murder in the frozen reserve of Wind River in an attempt to make amends for his previous failures.

Jeremy Renner portrays Cory Lambert, a US Fish and Wildlife Service tracker on the Wind River Indian Reservation in Wyoming, in what is possibly his greatest (and definitely most complex) performance to date. The appearance of Jane Banner (Elizabeth Olsen), a rookie FBI agent summoned by the Tribal Police, puts Lambert's hunting skills to the test. Banner who is extremely unprepared for the frosty wilderness of this remote reserve, immediately realizes that Lambert is her key to breaking the case, since he has a much better knowledge of the geography as well as the relationships among Wind River's indigenous population. Lambert, on the other hand, is considerably more concerned with exacting “wilderness” justice than with complying with federal law.

Wind River is a chilling film in more ways than one, with writer-director Taylor Sheridan combining suspense with social criticism in a narrative about individuals who have been left behind emotionally and financially. This film’s message regarding the living and policing conditions of the indigenous people in these remote reserves is perfectly conveyed through a bone-chilling and suspenseful tale. This film, engages the audience from the start and leaves them with something to contemplate afterwards.

The mystery here grows by the minute, propelled by outstanding screen chemistry between Renner, Olsen who are well known for their roles together in the Marvel Cinematic Universe.The score by Nick Cave and Warren Ellis is fantastic. The sound design makes excellent use of the loud silence of the icy winds of this remote land. There are plenty of moments in this film where the use of silence is very powerful in conveying the vast emptyness of Wind River. Additionally, there's plenty of action in this film too, but the film's investigation of race, gender, and sorrow is just as enthralling as any stand-off. Speaking of which, the film's final stand-off will have you holding your breath and feeling the shivers and chills of the freezing terrain of Wind River. 8.5/10.
